5 Bookkeeping Habits That Keep Minnesota Small Businesses Out of Trouble

Bookkeeping Tips · 4 min read

Most bookkeeping problems don't start as emergencies. They start small — a stack of uncategorized receipts, a bank account that hasn't been reconciled in two months, an invoice nobody followed up on. Left alone, those small things pile up into a stressful, expensive year-end scramble.

The good news: a handful of simple, consistent habits prevent almost all of it. Here are five we recommend to every small-business owner we work with.

1. Separate business and personal finances

If there's one habit that makes everything else easier, it's this. Keep a dedicated business checking account and a business card, and run every business transaction through them. Mixing personal and business spending creates hours of untangling later — and can cause real problems if you're ever audited.

2. Reconcile your accounts every month

Reconciling means matching your books to your actual bank and credit card statements. Doing it monthly catches errors, duplicate charges, and missing transactions while they're still easy to fix. Wait until year-end and you're reconstructing a year of activity from memory.

If your books and your bank statement don't agree, your books can't be trusted for decisions, loans, or taxes.

3. Stay on top of accounts receivable

Sending an invoice is only half the job — getting paid is the other half. Review your A/R report regularly, follow up on anything past due, and make it easy for customers to pay you. Cash flow problems are often just collection problems in disguise.

4. Code transactions consistently

Every transaction should land in the right expense account — and, when it matters, the right job or class. Consistent coding is what turns raw data into useful reports. It's the difference between "we spent a lot last month" and "materials costs on the Johnson project ran 12% over estimate."

5. Look at your numbers — every month

Clean books are only valuable if you actually use them. Set aside time each month to review your profit and loss, compare it to the prior month and the prior year, and ask what changed and why. Owners who do this spot problems early and make better decisions all year long.

The simplest habit of all

Hand the books to someone who does this every day. When bookkeeping is off your plate, these habits happen automatically — and you get accurate numbers, clear answers, and a lot less stress.
